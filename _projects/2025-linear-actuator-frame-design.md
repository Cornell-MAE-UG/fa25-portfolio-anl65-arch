As part of a homework assigment for my Statics and Mechanics of Solids course, I was asked to design a frame/mechanism that contained a linear actuator and could lift the maximum possible weight to the highest possible height, given a few restrictions. The frame/mechanism had to be limited to a 2D design space of 150cm (length) by 50cm (height), and had to contain a rigid bar of fixed length, 3 pin supports, and a linear actuator from the Tolomatic linear actuator online catalog (https://www.tolomatic.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/05/2700-4000_29_IMA_cat.pdf). The length of the rigid bar was a design choice I had to make and 2 of the 3 pin supports were required to be mounted to the ground.

Frame/Mechanism Design:({{ "/assets/images/linear-actuator-frame.jpg" | relative_url }}){: .inline-image-r style="width: 200px"}

For my frame/mechanism design, I chose to use the RSX linear actuator from the catalog as it provides the largest maximum force out of all of the other actuators, meaning it would be able to support the largest weight. Once I identified the RSX as my chosen actuator, I decided to create a triangular frame with a height of 50cm as the goal of the design was to lift the largest amount of weight, the highest possible height, and the height maximum is 50cm given the restricted 2D design space. The reason I chose to make the length of the rigid bar 140cm was because I wanted the linear actuator to be pinned at a large angle relative to the horizonital, so that most of the force it was providing went directly into supporting the load. To further elaborate, as the weight of the load provides a strict downward vertical force on the frame/mechanism, I deduced that it would be best and most efficient for the actuator to maximize its vertical force and minimize its horizontal force, necessitating a large angle between the actuator and the horizonal (as shown in the image above).
